Why I’m writing in public

A personal site can become a record of what you noticed, what you believed, and how your thinking changed.

I spend a lot of time following ideas down rabbit holes. A question about capitalism becomes a question about institutions. A question about software becomes a question about incentives. A historical event becomes a doorway into philosophy, religion, psychology, or politics.

Most of those thoughts disappear. They remain fragments in conversations, browser tabs, or notes that never become anything more coherent. This site is an attempt to change that.

Writing forces clarity

It is easy to feel that you understand something when the idea remains inside your own head. Writing exposes the gaps. It forces you to define terms, notice contradictions, and distinguish a real argument from an intuition that merely sounds persuasive.

I do not expect every post here to be definitive. In fact, I want the opposite: a living record of ideas being tested and refined. The goal is not to perform certainty. It is to become more precise over time.

A slower internet

Social platforms reward speed, compression, and reaction. A personal site offers a different rhythm. An idea can be longer than a caption, less polished than a book, and more durable than a post that vanishes into a feed.

I plan to write about technology, history, philosophy, faith, money, family, health, and whatever else seems important enough to think through carefully.

The point

This is not a declaration that I have answers. It is a commitment to take questions seriously—and to leave behind a trail that shows where those questions led.
